1160Challenges and Reasons for Optimism in 2023</strong>

1161</h4>

1162<p class="p light">

1163The survey also asked chefs to look ahead to 2023. <strong class="strong">

1164When asked what they anticipated being the biggest challenge for next year, respondents resoundingly spoke of staffing issues (“staff” or “labor” mentioned 198 times in open-ended answers), rising food costs and food shortages (127 mentions), inflation (39 mentions), and a looming recession (27 mentions). </strong>

1165Some feared burnout, while others expressed concerns over being able to grow their customer base from 2022. Answers around staffing spoke of a lack of a talent pool, people “not wanting to work,” increased labor costs, or a need to offer growing benefits to remain competitive with other local restaurants.</p>

1166<p class="p light">

1167Benefits and better working conditions, including better schedules, also appeared as reasons to be optimistic, with respondents being excited by changes to make restaurants more equitable and diverse workplaces. <strong class="strong">

1168“People are seeing the value in work-life balance,” </strong>

1169said one of the respondents. “People are seeing that we need to pay livable wages in this industry and understand why we charge more. People are paying more attention to discrimination and predatory practices.”</p>

1170<p class="p light">

1171The absence of COVID-19–related restrictions and a return to pre-2020 dining habits were more reasons to rejoice, with many respondents speaking to a return to normal. <strong class="strong">

1172And for all the labor-related woes, many respondents expressed hope in the “next generation” for motivating innovation and progress for the industry</strong>

1173.</p>

1174<p class="p light">

1175Chefs see customer habits as both a challenge and an opportunity for 2023. Many cited fears of people dining out less because their costs at home are increasing and they are looking to save money. On the flip side, respondents also repeatedly said that “everyone has to eat” and many people prefer that someone else do the cooking.</p>

1176<p class="p light">

1177<strong class="strong">

1178One final reason to be optimistic for 2023? The fortitude of the restaurant industry.</strong>

1179 “We are a resilient group of people who have fostered a strong network of community,” wrote in one of the respondents. Others wrote that they know the industry will make it through tough times the way it has so many times before.</p>

1180<p class="p light">

1181Having the right strategies in place will make a difference: “We have seen a lot of successes, too, which gives me hope. I still feel optimistic that we can get this back on track. Diversification is key here, we cannot rely on the old marketing tactics at this point,” wrote a respondent. <strong class="strong">

1182“Good food and service are incredibly important, but, we also have to start thinking more about what else we can offer or sell. </strong>

1183We are purveyors and the old model of out-sale your neighbor with a 10 for 10 deal is just undercutting all of us due to inflation, we need to start to think bigger and more broadly.”</p>
